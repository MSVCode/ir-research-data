Could climate change cause humans to SHRINK? Mammals became smaller due to global warming 54 million years ago

  • Researchers say that the 'dwarfing' was a natural adaptation to the heat
  • The animals lost some of their bulk to cope with climate-induced food shortages 
  • One expert claims that humans could shrink if the Earth went through a drastic rise in temperature

ANIMAL DWARFING 

Researchers have known for years that mammals such as primates and the groups that include horses and deer became much smaller during a period of warming.

This period is known as the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM), and occurred around 55 million years ago.

But the extent to which other ancient global warming events drove dwarfing has until now been unclear.

The new study shows that dwarfing is a natural evolutionary response to global warming events.

The results act as a warning to what current global warming could do to modern mammals.

The study suggests that the natural change in size seen in mammals is proportional, meaning the worse that global warming gets, the further mammals will shrink.

Two of the species found showed 'significant shrinkage' according to the study, with one - an early horse ancestor - getting 14 per cent smaller.

Global warming has led mammals to dwarf at least twice in Earth’s history, the researchers say.

Co-author Professor Philip Gingerich of the University of Michigan claims that, if Earth goes through similar temperature rises, humans could shrink.

'I would expect humans could shrink in size if we go through another hyperthermal like those in the early Eocene [period],' he told MailOnline.

'But we might be high enough in the food chain that we would be buffered.

'The dwarfing tends to affect herbivorous mammals more than carnivorous mammals,' he said.

Lead author Abigail Carroll claims that the effects of global warming are unlikely to change the size of humans.

'We, as humans, are very good at controlling the environment around us,' she told MailOnline.

'We’re good at find ways or seeking shelter to cool off, whether using air conditioning when it’s hot, or simply packing up and moving to a cooler place.

The worse that climate warming gets, the further mammals may shrink. Pictured are two horse ancestor tooth specimens. One lived during the warming event ETM-2 53.7 million years ago (left), while the other lived 2.5 million years later (right)

'So I guess I don’t expect the dwarfism response to manifest in humans the way it could for animals that have less control over their environments, like wildlife.'

The team's findings could have implications for other mammal species' response to current warming across the globe.

The authors propose several ways that warmer global temperatures could have triggered the animals' dwarfing.

A lack of available food thanks to the planet's changing climate is one idea put forward.

The animals could have evolved to be smaller so that they needed less food to survive.

'Climate change affects plants and animals in ways that are poorly understood,' the researchers say in their paper.

'Much can be learned from the study of climate change in the geological past.'

Researchers have known for years that mammals such as primates and the groups that include horses and deer became much smaller during a period of warming.

But the extent to which other ancient global warming events drove dwarfing has until now been unclear.

The new study shows that dwarfing is a natural evolutionary response to global warming events, and acts as a warning to what future global warming could bring to modern species.

The team analysed tooth enamel from the fossils of mammals that lived during a warming event around 53.7 million years ago.

The event, known as the Eocene Thermal Maximum 2 (ETM2), is the most recent bout of global temperature rises that we can compare to modern warming.

The fossils the team analysed were found buried in Bighorn Basin, Wyoming and represented four groups of animals.

The first was 'Arenahippus pernix', an early horse ancestor.

Teeth from 'Diacodexis metsiacus', an archaic rabbit-sized mammal related to cows and sheep; and 'Hyopsodus simplex', a weasel-like ancient mammal related to horses and rhinos, were also found.

 A lack of available food thanks to the planet's changing climate is one idea put forward as to why the animals shrunk. Pictured is the McCullough region of Wyoming's Bighorn Basin where most of the team's field sites were located

The animals could have evolved to be smaller so that they needed less food to survive. Pictured is a field site used by the team in Wyoming's Bighorn Basin. Fossil teeth are often found eroding out of the region's slopes

The final animal group represented by the teeth was 'Cantius abditus', an early primate similar to modern lemurs.

Using the teeth from the four groups the researchers could study the water and plantlife that the animals had eaten millions of years ago.

The teeth also acted as a proxy for estimating the body sizes of the ancient creatures.

D. metsiacus and A. pernix, which had the strongest fossil evidence, showed significant shrinkage during the warming event.

A. pernix shrank in body size by 14 per cent during ETM2, compared to a reported 30 per cent decrease in body size seen by close early horse relative 'Sifrhippus' during the more drastic PETM.

This suggests that shrinkage could be proportional to the extremity of the warming event.

The teeth were found in Bighorn Basin, a patch of land approximately 100 miles (160 km) wide in north-central Wyoming in the United States
